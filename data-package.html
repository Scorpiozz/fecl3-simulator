1. Introduction & Scope

This document is a complete engineering data package for the process simulation of chlorine gas (Cl₂) absorption into aqueous ferrous chloride (FeCl₂) solution to produce ferric chloride (FeCl₃). It contains every thermodynamic, kinetic, mass transfer, and physical property parameter required to build a rigorous process simulator from scratch.

1.1 Plant Description

  • Company: Chimi Art
  • Location: 6th October City, Industrial Zone, Giza, Egypt
  • Process: Two packed absorption towers in series with inter-stage cooling
  • Feed: ~34 wt% FeCl₂ aqueous solution (ρ ≈ 1380 kg/m³)
  • Product: ~40 wt% FeCl₃ aqueous solution (ρ ≈ 1415 kg/m³)
  • Cl₂ source: Liquid Cl₂ evaporator, 400 kg/h capacity
  • Mode: Currently batch; continuous operation planned

1.2 Document Purpose

This package is designed so that a chemical engineer with no prior knowledge of this specific system can:

  1. Understand the complete reaction chemistry and thermodynamics
  2. Reproduce the process simulation (discretized tower model with heat exchange)
  3. Evaluate thermal safety margins (HAW-H90 rubber lining limit: 100°C)
  4. Design packing heights for the two absorption towers
  5. Identify calibration priorities for plant commissioning
⚠️ Calibration Status: Two key parameters remain UNCALIBRATED and can only be determined from plant thermocouple data during commissioning: ΔHrxn (heat of reaction in concentrated solution) and HTU (height of transfer unit for our specific packing). All other parameters are validated from literature or vendor data.

2.2 Stoichiometric Relationships

  • Molar ratio: 2 mol FeCl₂ : 1 mol Cl₂ → 2 mol FeCl₃
  • Mass ratio: 253.5 g FeCl₂ + 70.91 g Cl₂ → 324.4 g FeCl₃
  • Cl₂ consumption: 0.280 kg Cl₂ per kg FeCl₂ converted

At 3 m³/h of 34% FeCl₂ (ρ = 1380 kg/m³):
m_feed = 3 × 1380 = 4140 kg/h
m_FeCl₂ = 4140 × 0.34 = 1407.6 kg/h = 11,105 mol/h
Stoichiometric Cl₂ = (11105 / 2) × 70.91 / 1000 = 393.7 kg/h
Electrochemistry: Fe²⁺ → Fe³⁺ (one-electron oxidation per iron atom). Cl₂ acts as oxidizer: Cl₂ + 2e⁻ → 2Cl⁻. Each Cl₂ molecule oxidizes 2 Fe²⁺ ions. No side reactions at pH < 2 (Cl₂ hydrolysis negligible — see Section 11.6).

3. Thermodynamics — Heat of Reaction (ΔHrxn)

The heat of reaction is the #1 design uncertainty in this system. The reaction is strongly exothermic, but the magnitude depends on whether the reaction occurs in solid state, dilute solution, or concentrated industrial solution.

3.1 Solid-State Estimate (Lower Bound)

ΔHrxn(solid) = 2 × ΔH°f(FeCl₃,s) − [2 × ΔH°f(FeCl₂,s) + 0] = −115.4 kJ/mol Cl₂

3.3 FeCl₂ Dissolution Enthalpy (Cross-Validation)

ΔHdiss(FeCl₂) = −82.9 kJ/mol (calorimetric, ScienceDirect)
Calculated: ΔH°f(Fe²⁺,aq) + 2×ΔH°f(Cl⁻,aq) − ΔH°f(FeCl₂,s) = −89.1 + 2(−167.2) − (−341.8) = −81.7 kJ/mol
Agreement within 1.5% confirms self-consistent thermodynamic cycle.

3.4 Concentrated Solution — Best Estimate

ΔHrxn(concentrated, ~34-40 wt%) ≈ −212 kJ/mol Cl₂ 🟡
Plausible range: −180 to −220 kJ/mol Cl₂
Method: 70% hydration correction between solid (−115) and infinite dilution (−253):
ΔH ≈ −115 + 0.70 × (253 − 115) = −212 kJ/mol Cl₂

At concentrated solutions (~34-40 wt%), ionic hydration shells are incomplete, so the actual ΔH lies between the solid and dilute limits. The 70% correction is an engineering estimate based on typical electrolyte behavior at high ionic strength.

⚠️ UNCALIBRATED: This is the single most impactful parameter in the simulation. A ±20 kJ/mol error in ΔH translates to ±3-5°C in sump temperature. Only plant thermocouple data (measuring ΔT across the tower at known Cl₂ flow) can pin this value. Priority #1 for commissioning.

4.3 Impact on Simulation

As the reaction progresses (FeCl₂ → FeCl₃), the total salt concentration increases from ~34% to ~40%, causing Cp to drop from ~2590 to ~2310 J/(kg·K) — a 10% decrease. This means less thermal inertia at the hot sump, resulting in approximately +2°C higher sump temperatures compared to constant-Cp models.

7. Reaction Kinetics — Hikita 1975

Primary source: Hikita, H. et al. (1975). "Kinetics of absorption of chlorine in aqueous acidic solutions of ferrous chloride." Chem. Eng. Sci., 30, 607-616 [1].

7.1 Rate Law

r = k₂ × [Cl₂] × [Fe²⁺]    (second order overall, first order in each reactant)

Where:
  r = reaction rate [mol/(L·s)]
  k₂ = second-order rate constant [L/(mol·s)]
  [Cl₂] = dissolved Cl₂ concentration [mol/L]
  [Fe²⁺] = ferrous ion concentration [mol/L]

7.2 Rate Constant Correlation (Equation 23 from Hikita)

log₁₀(k₂) = 9.633 + 0.123 × I − 2200 / T

Where:
  k₂ in L/(gmol·s)
  I = ionic strength in g-ion/L
  T = absolute temperature in K

7.3 Activation Energy

Ea = 42.1 kJ/mol (10,050 cal/mol)

⚠️ Extrapolation Warning: The Hikita correlation was validated for I = 0.3–3.0 g-ion/L and T = 15–45°C. Our system operates at I ≈ 11 and T up to 80°C. The ionic strength term (0.123 × I) dominates at high I, but the physical meaning is unclear outside the tested range. However, since Ha >> 1 regardless, the exact k₂ value barely affects results.

7.5 Ionic Strength Calculation

I = ½ × Σ(ci × zi²)

For 34% FeCl₂ (ρ = 1380 kg/m³):
  cFeCl₂ = (0.34 × 1380) / 126.75 = 3.70 mol/L
  [Fe²⁺] = 3.70 mol/L, z = +2
  [Cl⁻] = 2 × 3.70 = 7.40 mol/L, z = −1
  I = ½ × (3.70 × 4 + 7.40 × 1) = 11.1 mol/L

8.3 Sechenov Salting-Out Effect

log₁₀(C*₀ / C*) = ks × I

Where:
  C*₀ = Cl₂ solubility in pure water
  C* = Cl₂ solubility in salt solution
  ks(FeCl₂) = 0.0157 L/g-ion at 25°C (Hikita, Table 3) [1]
  Temperature correction: ks(T) ≈ 0.0157 + (TC − 25) × (−0.00046)
  I = ionic strength in g-ion/L

At our conditions (I ≈ 11): salting-out factor = 10(0.0157 × 11) = 1.49 → Cl₂ solubility drops 33% compared to pure water.

Practical relevance: In the fast reaction regime (Ha >> 1), physical solubility has limited direct impact because Cl₂ reacts at the gas-liquid interface before reaching bulk equilibrium. However, it affects the driving force calculation and the Hatta number through the interfacial concentration Ci.

9. Mass Transfer — HTU, NTU, Hatta Number

9.1 Hatta Number

Ha = √(k₂ × DA × [Fe²⁺]) / kL

At C1 sump conditions (T ≈ 80°C, 34% FeCl₂):
  k₂ ≈ 5000 L/(mol·s) (extrapolated at I ≈ 11)
  DA ≈ 5.75 × 10⁻⁶ cm²/s (Stokes-Einstein corrected)
  [Fe²⁺] ≈ 3.7 mol/L
  kL ≈ 0.015 cm/s (typical for random packing)
  Ha ≈ 22.6

9.2 Absorption Regime

Ha RangeRegimeControlling ResistanceOur System
Ha < 0.3Very slowBulk liquid
0.3 < Ha < 3SlowLiquid film
Ha > 3FastGas filmHa ≈ 22.6 ✓

Since Ha >> 3, the reaction is fast (gas-film controlled). All Cl₂ reacts within the liquid film before reaching the bulk. This means:

  • HTU ≈ HTUG (gas-side transfer unit height dominates)
  • Enhancement factor E ≈ Ha ≈ 22.6
  • Liquid-side resistance is negligible

9.3 HTU (Height of Transfer Unit)

HTUG ≈ 0.35 m   (for 50 mm PP Pall rings at G ≈ 0.024 m/s) 🟡
Plausible range: 0.25 – 0.60 m

NTU = Hpack / HTU
  C1 (6 m packing): NTU = 6 / 0.35 = 17.1
  C2 (3 m packing): NTU = 3 / 0.35 = 8.6

Estimated from Onda et al. (1968) correlation for gas-side mass transfer in random packing [15]. The low gas velocity means gas-phase Reynolds number is low — HTU is conservative.

⚠️ UNCALIBRATED: HTU is the #2 uncertainty after ΔHrxn. For our specific packing (type, size, material TBD) and our specific gas/liquid rates, the actual HTU can only be determined from plant Cl₂ slip measurements. A 2× difference in HTU changes conversion and Cl₂ distribution but has modest impact on temperature (~±5°C).

11. Vapor-Liquid Equilibria (VLE)

This section covers all gas-liquid equilibrium relationships in the FeCl₂/FeCl₃/Cl₂/HCl/H₂O system that affect the simulation.

11.1 Water Activity (aW) — Modified Raoult's Law

Dissolved electrolytes depress the vapor pressure of water. The water activity is computed from the mole fraction of water accounting for complete ionic dissociation:

FeCl₂ → Fe²⁺ + 2 Cl⁻    (i = 3 ions per formula unit)
FeCl₃ → Fe³⁺ + 3 Cl⁻    (i = 4 ions per formula unit)

aW = nwater / (nwater + nions)

Where:
  nwater = mwater(kg) × 1000 / 18.015   [mol]
  nions = nFeCl₂ × 3 + nFeCl₃ × 4   [mol]
ConditionMolality (m)aW (ideal)VP Depression
34% FeCl₂ (feed)4.060.82018%
Mid-tower (~17% FeCl₂ / 20% FeCl₃)—0.79620%

Note: These are ideal Raoult's law values. FeCl₃ osmotic coefficients from Robinson & Stokes [12] show φ > 1 at high molality, meaning the real aW is LOWER (more VP depression). Our ideal calculation is therefore a conservative (upper bound) estimate of water activity.

11.4 HCl Vapor Pressure

HCl vapor pressure over FeCl₃ solutions is negligible below 100°C. The HCl-water azeotrope occurs at 20.2 wt% HCl / 110°C. At our conditions (trace HCl only, T < 100°C), HCl remains entirely in solution.

11.5 Cl₂ Hydrolysis

Cl₂(aq) + H₂O ⇌ HOCl + H⁺ + Cl⁻
Kh = 3.94 × 10⁻⁴ mol²/L² at 25°C

At pH < 2 (our conditions with high [Cl⁻] and [H⁺]), the equilibrium strongly favors dissolved molecular Cl₂. Hydrolysis is negligible — less than 0.1% of dissolved Cl₂ converts to HOCl.

11.6 Impact on Simulation

  • Water evaporation rate is 20-30% lower than pure-water Antoine predicts
  • Temperature impact is small (~0.2°C) because the heat pipe effect is internal redistribution, not net cooling
  • BPE confirms the HAW-H90 100°C limit is the binding thermal constraint
  • HCl and Cl₂ hydrolysis: negligible at our conditions

12. Water Evaporation Model

12.1 Physical Description

Dry Cl₂ gas enters the tower bottom from the evaporator. As it rises through the packed bed, it contacts hot liquid and picks up water vapor. The gas becomes progressively saturated with water as it ascends.

12.2 "Heat Pipe" Effect

Key insight: Water evaporation in packed towers acts as an internal heat pipe:
• Hot sump (bottom): Water evaporates from liquid → absorbs latent heat (cooling effect)
• Cool top: Water condenses from gas → releases latent heat (heating effect)
• Net result: Heat is redistributed from bottom to top, but not removed from the system
• Net water loss from the tower is negligible (~0.1 kg/h)

12.3 Equations

ΔHvap(H₂O) = 40,700 J/mol

Water vapor mole fraction at segment i:
yw,i = aW,i × Psat(Ti) / Ptotal   (capped at 0.80 for numerical stability)

Water vapor carried by gas:
vapori = [yw,i / (1 − yw,i)] × max(0.1, Cl₂i)   [mol/h]

Water transfer per segment:
Δwateri = vapori − vapori−1   [mol/h]
  Positive = evaporation (liquid → gas, cooling)
  Negative = condensation (gas → liquid, heating)

15. Simulation Mathematical Model

This section describes the complete mathematical model in sufficient detail to code the simulation from scratch in any programming language.

15.1 Model Architecture

  • Type: Discretized counter-current packed absorption tower
  • Segments: Nseg = 20 per tower
  • Segment height: dz = Hpack / Nseg
  • Indexing: i = 0 (sump/bottom) to Nseg (top)
  • Gas flows UP (i = 0 → Nseg): Cl₂ enters at i = 0, exits at i = Nseg
  • Liquid flows DOWN (i = Nseg → 0): Feed enters at i = Nseg, product exits at i = 0

15.2 Iteration Scheme

For iter = 1 to 80:
  1. Gas-Up Pass (i = 0 → Nseg): Compute Cl₂ absorption + water vapor
  2. Liquid-Down Pass (i = Nseg−1 → 0): Compute mass balance + energy balance
  3. Apply under-relaxation on temperatures

Under-relaxation: Tinew = α × Ticalc + (1 − α) × Tiold
  α = 0.35 (prevents oscillation from latent heat feedback)

15.3 Gas-Up Pass — Cl₂ Absorption

Absorption fraction per segment:
αseg = 1 − exp(−dz / HTU)

Rate-limited absorption at segment i:
ratei = αseg × Cl₂i−1 × FeCl₂i / (FeCl₂i + 0.5 × αseg × Cl₂i−1 + ε)

Where ε = 10⁻³ (prevents division by zero)

Rate caps (stoichiometric and physical limits):
ratei = min(ratei, 0.95 × Cl₂i−1, 0.475 × FeCl₂i)

Gas phase update:
Cl₂i = max(0, Cl₂i−1 − ratei)

15.4 Gas-Up Pass — Water Vapor

Water activity at segment i:
aW,i = nwater,i / (nwater,i + 3 × nFeCl₂,i + 4 × nFeCl₃,i)
  where nwater,i = mwater,i(kg/h) × 1000 / 18.015 [mol/h]

Saturated water vapor mole fraction:
yw,i = min(0.80, aW,i × Psat(Ti) / 101325)

Water vapor carried by gas:
vapori = [yw,i / max(0.01, 1 − yw,i)] × max(0.1, Cl₂i)   [mol/h]

Water transfer:
Δwater0 = vapor0   (gas enters dry at bottom)
Δwateri = vapori − vapori−1   (i ≥ 1)

15.5 Liquid-Down Pass — Mass Balance

For i = Nseg−1 down to 0:

FeCl₂i = max(0, FeCl₂i+1 − 2 × ratei)   [mol/h]
FeCl₃i = FeCl₃i+1 + 2 × ratei   [mol/h]
mwater,i = max(0.1, mwater,i+1 − Δwateri × 18.015 / 1000)   [kg/h]

Local composition:
msalt,i = FeCl₂i × 126.75/1000 + FeCl₃i × 162.20/1000   [kg/h]
mtotal,i = msalt,i + mwater,i   [kg/h]
wsalt%,i = (msalt,i / mtotal,i) × 100

15.6 Liquid-Down Pass — Energy Balance

Local heat capacity:
Cpi = 4186 − 47 × wsalt%,i + 0.1 × (Tiold − 25)   [J/(kg·K)] (if variable Cp enabled)
  or Cpi = Cpfixed (if constant Cp mode)

Heat generation:
Qrxn,i = (ratei / 3600) × ΔHrxn   [W]

Latent heat (evaporation/condensation):
Qwater,i = (Δwateri / 3600) × 40700   [W]
  (positive = evaporation/cooling, negative = condensation/heating)

Net heat to liquid:
Qnet,i = Qrxn,i − Qwater,i   [W]

Heat capacity rate:
Clocal,i = mtotal,i × Cpi / 3600   [W/K]

Temperature (before relaxation):
Ticalc = Ti+1 + Qnet,i / max(Clocal,i, 1)

15.7 Heat Exchanger Model (ε-NTU Method)

Flow-dependent overall heat transfer coefficient:
UAactual = UAdesign × (F / Fdesign)0.7
  UAdesign = 10,375 W/K, Fdesign = 10 m³/h

Capacitance rates:
Cproc = (mfeed × Cp) / 3600   [W/K]
Ccw = (Fcw × 1000 × 4180) / 3600   [W/K]

Cmin = min(Cproc, Ccw),   Cmax = max(Cproc, Ccw)
Cr = Cmin / Cmax,   NTU = UAactual / Cmin

Effectiveness (counter-flow):
ε = [1 − exp(−NTU × (1 − Cr))] / [1 − Cr × exp(−NTU × (1 − Cr))]
  (capped at 0.98; for Cr < 0.01: ε = 1 − exp(−NTU))

Duty and outlet temperature:
Q = ε × Cmin × (Thot,in − Tcw)   [W]
Thot,out = Thot,in − Q / Cproc

15.8 Two-Tower Cascade

Step 1: C1 tower simulation
  Input: Feed (34% FeCl₂, Tfeed) + Cl₂ (FCl₂ × split)
  Output: Partially converted liquor at TC1,sump

Step 2: HX-301 (inter-stage cooler)
  Input: C1 product at TC1,sump
  Output: Cooled to Tafter,HX1

Step 3: C2 tower simulation
  Input: Cooled C1 product + Cl₂ (FCl₂ × (1−split))
  Output: Final product at TC2,sump

Step 4: HX-401 (product cooler)
  Input: C2 product at TC2,sump
  Output: Cooled product at Tafter,HX2

18.2 Key Findings

ΔHrxn dominates everything. A ±20 kJ/mol uncertainty in ΔH causes ±3-5°C swing in sump temperature. Getting plant thermocouple data to back-calculate ΔH is the #1 commissioning priority.

Calibration method: Measure Tin and Tout of one tower at known Cl₂ and liquid flow. Back-calculate ΔH from: Q = m × Cp × ΔT, where Q = nCl₂ consumed × ΔH.

18.3 Cl₂ Split Optimization

The 50/50 Cl₂ split is not necessarily optimal. Since C2 is smaller (Ø900 vs Ø1000), sending less Cl₂ to C2 could balance temperatures. A 60/40 split sends more Cl₂ to C1 (which has better cooling due to larger thermal mass and more HEX capacity).
